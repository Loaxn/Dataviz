Our website is a university project that focuses on data visualization, centering on the critical issue of drug overdoses in the United States. Specifically, we have chosen to explore the trends and impacts associated with overdoses related to three significant drugs: heroin, cocaine, and methadone.

We obtained trustworthy data for our project on drug overdoses in the United States from external websites. These sources have been carefully selected to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the information we present.

Heroin

Heroin is an opioid drug made from morphine, a natural substance taken from the seed pod of the various opium poppy plants grown in Southeast and Southwest Asia, Mexico, and Colombia. Heroin can be a white or brown powder.

Heroin was synthesized in 1874 and commercialized by Bayer in 1898. Its addictive nature led to regulation in 1914. After a decline post-World War II, there was a resurgence in the 1960s and 1970s. Heroin abuse persisted, linked to poverty and the drug trade. In the late 20th century to the present, it's part of the opioid epidemic, addressed through law enforcement and addiction treatment efforts.

Cocaine

Cocaine, a potent stimulant from the coca plant, is used medically but illegal recreationally.

Its U.S. abuse history began in the late 19th century, leading to regulations like the 1914 Harrison Act.
Resurging in the late 20th century, especially in the 1970s and 1980s, the rise of crack cocaine intensified problems.
Addressing abuse involves law enforcement, public health campaigns, and treatment initiatives.
Cocaine remains a controlled substance due to recognized addiction risks and adverse health effects.

Methadone

Methadone is an opioid medication often used to treat opioid addiction.
Unfortunately, many people misuse the drug itself, which can still cause a euphoric high and other dangerous side effects.

Methadone is one of the most commonly abused opioids in the U.S. and is mainly used to help people overcome physical dependence on heroin and morphine.
The abuse of methadone has occurred over the years,
and it became more notable in the late 20th and early 21st centuries as part of the broader opioid epidemic in the United States.
